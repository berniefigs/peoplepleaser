Social media has not been around long enough for a sufficient amount
of research to come forth on a verdict on if its overall effect deems to be
damaging or not. Social media is constantly changing and evolving, which
entails that there has not been any long term studies executed. Nothing good
ever comes out of excess, especially something that is boundless.

In 2006, a podcasting company, Odeo needed a fresh, new idea to reinvent
themselves. Jack Dorsey introduced the idea of creating SMS (short message
service, a.k.a. texting) that would enable a user to communicate with a small
group of people. Then there’s Mark Zuckerberg, who created Facebook to
connect with people. At the time the Internet was only useful for information
and services, but no real way to connect people together virtually. So you
could say social media was not born in vain, especially when these CEO’s
prioritized the value of social connection. Social media is also great for
introverts, shy folks, communities, and long distance relationships. It is
great for keeping friends and family updated on our new life circumstances.
There is insufficient amount of data to prove its long term effects, so we
cannot set any kind of guidelines or regulations yet. But investing in social
media too frivolously can eventually result in harmful impact, like caring
about the wrong things that do not actually matter in real life. But with an
increase in screen time and decrease in IRL (in real life) time, empathy and
compassion start to diminish due to lack of real life social interaction. We
haven’t even discussed the horrors of cyberbullying, but that’s another can
of worms to breakdown. I worry that this type of technology could be
dangerous to a user who isn’t self-assured in oneself.

get when you post something and receive a ton of feedback? For every like we
receive, we get a high, a shot of dopamine and we are in a loop. It is so addictive

One small study out of the University of Pittsburgh concluded that there is in fact a
causal link between the use of social media and negative effects on well-being,
primarily depression and loneliness. The study was published in the Journal of Social
and Clinical Psychology.
“What we found overall is that if you use less social media, you are actually less
depressed and less lonely, meaning that the decreased social media use is what
causes that qualitative shift in your well-being,” said Jordyn Young, a co-author
of the paper and a senior at the University of Pennsylvania.

The researchers mention that this is the first time a causal link has ever been
established in scientific research.

SELFIE DYSMORPHIA
SELF-OBJECTIFICATION
+ PSYCHOLOGICAL DISTRESS

Back in 2017, the United Kingdom’s Royal Society for Public
Health published #StatusOfMind, a report that examined how
different social media platforms impacted people’s mental or
physical health. Almost 1,500 teens and young adults
participated, and Instagram got the worst rep, being ranked
as the worst app for your health. It was associated with high
levels of anxiety, depression, bullying, loneliness, sleep
quality, FOMO and body image.

Instagram was designed for creative and expressive purpose
of image sharing. In the beginning no one really took it
seriously, until a few years ago. I blame the intertwinement
of DTC (direct to consumer) brands and social media, which
resulted in a chaotic evil. Influencer marketing has always
been around, celebrities endorsing products on commercials or
magazine ads. But the birth of the DTC era exacerbated the
influencer pool to new heights. Through social media influencers
can advertise directly to the consumer. It starts to get
personal, because people get to see this influencer’s
transparent, authentic self. Then users trust their endorse-
ments; a subtle yet impactful method of persuasion to sell a
product. Instagram is misleading for this very reason: that
people are showcasing their perceived private lives on a public
platform; so the question how authentic is it, or are people
just constructing an online persona?

Instagram is overruled with influencers, really attractive
internet celebrities. The Instagram influencer market is
projected to reach a value of $2 billion globally by this
year, according to media marketing agency MediaKix.
Specifically, influencers in the fitness field are making the
big bucks on the Instagram platform. Users source “fitspiration”
for get inspired and motivated, which is a fitness influencer
whose content consists of sometimes tasteful half naked photos,
lifestyle and workout videos. Research shows this has the opposite
effect, making people feel worse and does not in fact correlate to
an increase in exercise or health outcomes. Considering the height
of celebrities and influencers dominating Instagram, it makes these
beautiful strangers an everyday person in your virtual reality,
giving more opportunity for comparison as a user scrolls through
their timeline. With body dissatisfaction at an all time high, people
are starting to rely on face slimming face filters and Facetune to
alter how they look.

“If you think about all these apps you can use now with filters and
manipulating the body, it’s almost like you’ve got Photoshop in your
phone. You are literally manipulating or photoshopping your image to
conform with social norms, to be evaluated by others,” said Cohen.

Some people get so used to how they look on Facetune or Snapchat or
Instagram filters that it is driving them to seek surgery. We're
currently living in a phenomenon of people requesting procedures to
resemble their digital image which is termed as “selfie dysmorphia”
or Snapchat dysmorphia. Where is the fine line between a photo filter
from VSCO and straight up changing the appearance of your face
structure? A report in the US medical journal JAMA Facial Plastic Surgery
suggested that filtered images’ blur the lines of reality and fantasy, in
which could potentially be triggering body dimorphic disorder (BDD), a
mental health condition where people become fixated on imagined defects
in their appearance.

In 2017, a study in the journal Cognitive Research: Principles and
Implications found that people only recognized manipulates images
60-65% of the time, causing “unrealistic expectations of what is
normal” and has the potential to destroy one’s self esteem. It
becomes a vivacious cycling of getting insecure while scrolling
through Instagram, then manipulating images, then someone seeing
the manipulated image.. completely unaware that it has been altered,
feel like shit about themselves.

The issue with Snapchat and Instagram filters are they get rid of
any sign of human. They plump your lips, erase your pores, accentuate
your jawline, and making your eyes pop.

Fillers have become so prevalent, which leads to bigger procedures.
Some doctors even tried to market off of Kylie Jenner’s lip fillers
with this “Kylie package: nose, jaw, and lips”. It's not taboo any-
more to see doctors post the work they have done on patients to
advertise. Instagram has become a marketplace for cosmetic surgery.

Most recently Instagram banned some selfie filters that mimic plastic
surgery, claiming it to be damaging to teens. On the bright side this ban
is part of new efforts by Instagram to hunt down content related to dieting,
detoxes and domestic surgery. It’s sad for young girls to grow up in this
era, feeling as if their natural state will never compare to their air-
brushed selfie filter face. Influencers have a huge platform and should be
using it to influence positive reinforcements, considering how much power
they hold nowadays to shift pop culture.
